Why This Matters

Research Background

The Problem

Pain is one of the most common reasons people seek medical care, and it's still hard to treat well. Medication, physical therapy, and invasive procedures all work in some cases, but each carries side effects, high costs, or dependency risks.

Why Music?

Music costs nothing, requires no prescription, and doesn't cause side effects. Some prior research points in a positive direction, but tightly controlled tests are rare. We wanted to add a small, concrete data point.

Research Context

Music therapy and psychoacoustics research suggested that music can reduce perceived pain. We designed a study to test whether it changed pain intensity ratings and how long people could tolerate discomfort.

The Gap

Anecdotal and some clinical evidence points to music helping with pain, but few studies test this with a standardised cold-pressor method that students can actually run. We built a simple, repeatable version of that test.

Our Focus

Research Questions

Q1

Does music reduce self-reported pain intensity during a cold-pressor discomfort task?

Q2

Does music increase pain tolerance time during a cold-pressor discomfort task?

Project Vision

If music reliably reduces perceived pain and extends tolerance, it could be a cheap, useful addition to pain management. Not a replacement for medical treatment, but something anyone can try.

How We Did It

Experiment Procedure

15 Participants
2 Conditions
2 Time Points
1 Survey
1

Study Design

A within-subjects comparison with two conditions: music and a neutral control. Each participant completed both, so we could compare their responses head-to-head.

2

Cold Pressor Task

Participants submerged one hand in cold water for up to 60 seconds. This standardised method is common in pain research and produces real but tolerable discomfort.

3

Data Collection

Pain intensity was self-rated at 30 seconds and 60 seconds. Total time in the water was recorded for each condition.

4

Post-Task Survey

After each session, participants filled out a short survey on their background, how they perceived the pain, and how effective they found the music.

What This Means

The numbers point in the same direction: music made pain feel less bad. Participants weren't pain-free, but most said the music session was easier, with lower reported distress and longer tolerance times.

So What?

Implications

Music Shows Promise

Our findings suggest that music can help with pain in a real, measurable way. It's free, needs no prescription, and works through earbuds most people already own.

Tolerance May Improve

Music appears to help people stay in discomfort longer. This might work through distraction, emotional regulation, or both.

Subjective Experience Shifts

Even when the physical conditions are the same, music changes how pain feels. That shift in experience matters, even if the cold water temperature doesn't change.

Not a Universal Cure

These results are promising for a 15-person study, but they're not enough to change how doctors treat pain. That would take much larger, more diverse trials.

Honest Assessment

Limitations & Future Work

Limitations

  • Small sample size — Only 15 participants, limiting statistical power.
  • No music preference control — Participants did not choose their music.
  • Simulated pain — Cold-pressor discomfort differs from clinical pain.
  • Environmental variation — Minor setting or context changes may have influenced results.

Future Directions

  • Larger, diverse sample — Increase participant count and demographic range.
  • Genre comparison — Test whether music preference or genre affects outcomes.
  • Physiological measures — Add heart rate, skin conductance, or cortisol data.
  • Realistic pain settings — Test in clinical or post-operative environments.
